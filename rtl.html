RTL (right-to-left)
Design Guideline

2019 is another important year for AliExpress to go further with it's global business. And this year, we are aiming for markets where people read from right-to-left(RTL).

What is RTL?

RTL means languages that are read from right-to-left (RTL), such as Arabic and Hebrew. When the design for UIs, languages should be mirrored to ensure content is easy to understand.

The main difference between left-to-right (LTR) and right-to-left (RTL) language scripts is the direction in which content is displayed:

• LTR languages display content from left to right
• RTL languages display content from right to left

Why RTL matter?

There are almost 400 million RTL users all over the world (From Apple WWDC16, Session 232). 12 listed languages that are written from the right to the left: Arabic, Aramaic, Azeri, Dhivehi, Fula, Hebrew, Kurdish (Sorani), N’ko,Persian, Rohingya, Syriac, Urdu.

RTL may not be the first thing you think about when you think about user experience. But these numbers make it clear, there are the people that use your app and creating designs that work for them is at the heart of the user experience.


Where are these RTL Countries located?

There are many countries use RTL languages. These main areas are included: Afghanistan, Israel, Middle East, Pakistan



Major Steps to design for RTL users

Here are three major steps to Localize for the RTL languages
1. Reverse the sequence for a series of events
2. Translate the text and numbers by making them localizable labels instead of being baked into art
3. Mirror certain items to make it feel natural to read from Right to Left

Translate the text and numbers

Translate those text that can be translated. A word such as AliExpress can not be translated into the local language can stay as an untranslated text (even if it’s part of a phrase).

Define mirrored and not mirrored items

When a UI changes from one direction to another, these items are not mirrored: Numerals, Phone Numbers, Clocks, Music Notes, Video Playback Controls, Charts and Graphs, Images, Untranslated text (even if it’s part of a phrase)

Icon

One of the tasks of icons is to help users quickly identify something, or action regardless of their language. Therefore mirroring icons unnecessarily could actually fall into the counterproductive side of the field.

When we are thinking about a mirrored icon, we think about the meaning behind it. If the icon has a strong relationship with the physical world, or let’s say it’s what it’s. Then don’t mirror it and let it be. Icons that are symmetrical and that don’t point in a particular direction should not be flipped either. Listed below are the kinds of icons that shouldn’t be mirrored: Holding with one’s right hand, Symmetrical, Physical objects, Containing non-RTL characters, Slashes, Clockwise.

Using icons in RTL design can be tricky. Keep in mind that some of them might have to be mirrored, and some could be considered offensive to people of some nationalities, so double-check that the icons you’re using make sense.

Font

Here are some major parts that we want to talk about when we talk about font: Font weight, font length，font decoration and line height.



Font Weight

Try to avoid using bold, italics and capital. Bold text would make readability extremely difficult in most RTL languages (especially Arabic), and italics is simply never used. Font Length

Words in RTL languages are often much shorter than words in English. So, adjust to keep a balance in how text is displayed on the page.

Line Height

The line height of Arabic is tricky though. We should always pay attention to the extra marks which are called discritics, vowel marks, or tone marks. And sometimes they’re extremely high or low. For the Arabic and Hindi fonts, they have descenders much lower than Latin characters.

Color

Color is another fundamental design element. There’s a lot to consider when choosing colors for the design because they will bring emotions to the experience. Yellow represents death in Saudi Arabia. Purple and pink may not be a good choice when we design for Yemen.

Calendar

Calendars are probably one of the most important and complicated aspects of RTL design because calendar years are different between LTR and RTL geographic regions. Let’s just take Arabic as an example. Calendar in Arabic read from Right to Left just as the normal layout.
